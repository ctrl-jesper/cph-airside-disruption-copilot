# CPH Apron Optimizer: Solution Overview and Assumptions

**Purpose.** This document describes the demo solution built for the "AI Business Lead, Operations" case. It states what the solution is, what it genuinely computes versus what it simulates, and the scope decisions and assumptions made to keep the proof of concept honest and demonstrable. It is the source material for the solution introduction in the presentation.

**Prepared:** 2026-06-08. **Demo:** 2026-06-12.

---

## 1. The solution in one sentence

A hybrid decision-support copilot for Airside Operations stand allocation, human-in-the-loop and read-only, built from a deterministic optimization core, a machine-learning timing layer, a digital twin, and a conversational interface. The system proposes, a person decides, and nothing is written back to airport systems automatically.

## 2. A short introduction you can lift

The problem is a busy afternoon where a central stand fails, aircraft arrive off-slot, and one ground handler cannot service every turnaround at once. The decisions interact across stands, passport control, baggage, handlers, and passenger connections, so the best choice in one place creates a problem in another, and the consequences are not always visible at the moment of decision. Today this is handled manually, and the knowledge is person-bound.

The solution is a decision-support copilot that re-optimizes the whole apron in one pass, lays out the genuine trade-offs rather than hiding them in a single answer, and projects a chosen plan forward in time to reveal its second-order effects before anyone commits to it. It computes recommendations with a real optimizer, not a language model, and it keeps the human in control: the tool proposes and explains, the duty manager accepts, and every committed assignment is checked continuously against the live picture.

## 3. Scope

- **In scope.** Real-time stand re-allocation across the apron during disruption, with the knock-on effects for passport control (Schengen and non-Schengen), baggage belts, ground handlers, and passenger connections. The full-scale build adds tow-to-remote (parking a long-dwell aircraft remotely to free its contact stand mid-dwell) with tug and bus fleet capacity, arrival-time uncertainty handling, a bottom-up value figure, and a rolling re-optimization horizon.
- **Out of scope in this build.** Crew availability and positioning, landside and terminal operations beyond passport and connections, and any airport-wide optimization outside Airside Operations.
- **The decision split.** Stand allocation is the airport's own decision, so the AI is proactive there. Divert or cancel is the airline's call with ATC and the Network Manager, so the AI detects when no feasible stand exists, quantifies the knock-on, and escalates the trade-off. It never cancels or diverts a flight itself.

## 4. Architecture, the layers

| Layer | Role | What it is | Deterministic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Live feed (data) | The operating picture | Read-only feed from Airhart (the AODB), with handler, baggage, and connection data | n/a |
| ML predictive layer | The inputs: realistic timings | A model trained on historical A-CDM data, predicting on-block, off-block, and turnaround times with uncertainty | No, probabilistic |
| Optimization engine | The breadth: feasibility and whole-apron allocation | An OR solver (MIP or CP). Hard constraints filter what is legal; weighted soft costs rank what is good | Yes |
| Prioritizer (Pareto) | The trade-off: the genuine choices | An epsilon-constraint sweep over the same MIP across three objectives, returning the non-dominated strategies | Yes |
| Digital twin | The depth over time: the cascade | A forward simulation that projects a chosen allocation and reveals its second-order effects | Yes |
| Copilot interface (LLM) | The explanation: plain language and conversation | A language model that narrates the engine's output, captures tacit operator rules, and takes open questions. It explains; it never computes or allocates | Narration only |
| Governance | The control: review, accept, revalidate, audit | Human-in-the-loop. A person accepts a proposal; every committed assignment is revalidated continuously; everything is logged | n/a |

**The division of labour.** The ML layer gives the inputs (realistic times). The solver gives the breadth (the best trade-offs across the whole apron now). The digital twin gives the depth (the same allocation projected forward to expose the cascade). The recommendation is always computed by the deterministic engine, never generated by the language model.

## 5. What is real versus simulated in the demo

The demo's credibility rests on being clear about this line.

### Genuinely computed (real)

- **The optimization engine.** A real mixed-integer solver, glpk.js (GLPK compiled to WebAssembly), runs locally in the browser and solves the whole-apron assignment in roughly 10 to 40 milliseconds. The allocation and its costs are computed and inspectable, not scripted.
- **The Pareto trade-off.** A real epsilon-constraint sweep over the same model produces the non-dominated front and the three signature strategies. These are solver outputs, not hand-picked numbers.
- **The digital twin.** A real discrete forward simulation computes the passport queues, handler load, baggage belt load, connection risk, and any turnaround slip, step by step over the afternoon.
- **The human-in-the-loop loop.** The accept-and-commit step, the continuous revalidation sweep, and the event log are real state logic, verified end to end.
- **EK151.** The centerpiece is the real Emirates A380 service from Dubai to Copenhagen, which genuinely requires a code-F stand. This is a checkable flight.

### Simulated or placeholder (synthetic), labelled as such in the app

- **The ML timing layer.** The predicted on-block times, the uncertainty bands, and the turnaround durations are hand-set values per flight. There is no trained model and no A-CDM data ingested. The app labels them synthetic and "standing in for" a trained model. Section 8 describes the production approach.
- **The live Airhart feed.** Represented by a ticking synthetic clock, not a live integration.
- **The flight data.** A synthetic AODB snapshot. The identity layer (carrier, route, aircraft type) uses real CPH services; the mechanics layer (times, buffers, flags) is illustrative. Flight numbers other than EK151 are realistic but not claimed as specific scheduled services.
- **The outbound flights and the connection mapping.** The six departures in the connection bank, and the share of connecting passengers feeding each, are synthetic, present to make the passenger-flow Sankey meaningful.
- **The cost units.** The three soft costs are dimensionless points, not calibrated business value. They capture the shape of the trade-off correctly but not its real-world magnitude.
- **The operational parameters.** Passport processing rates, handler capacities, baggage belt capacities, walking-time proxies, and the connection overhead are illustrative figures chosen to make the scenario reproducible.

## 6. Key assumptions and modelling decisions

- **Recommendations are computed, not generated.** All decision logic is a deterministic hard-constraint filter plus a weighted score, solved by the MIP. The language model only narrates. This is the answer to "is this just a chatbot."
- **Handler capacity is a soft pressure, not a hard rule.** A stretched handler raises the cost and is flagged for human coordination; it does not block an assignment. This matches the operational reality where the duty manager and the handler agree a workaround. The injectable "Aviator over capacity" scenario demonstrates it.
- **Stand availability and aircraft size are hard constraints.** A stand that is out of service, already occupied in the relevant window, or too small for the aircraft is simply not offered.
- **The handler base-distance term.** Handler cost depends on the stand because handlers stage crews and equipment in apron zones, so servicing far from base adds drive time and vehicle cycles. This is what makes "protect handlers" a genuinely distinct strategy rather than a copy of "protect schedule."
- **Walking distance is in the model.** A flight's passengers reach passport control at on-block plus the stand's walking time, so a remote stand both arrives late and lands its full load at once. This drives the passport queue and the connection-risk test.
- **The connection-risk test.** A connecting passenger is at risk when walking time, plus the modelled passport wait, plus a fixed 15-minute overhead for security re-clearance and the walk to the gate, exceeds the flight's tightest connection.
- **The digital twin is the depth tool.** The snapshot optimizer places aircraft for the picture as it is now; the twin runs the clock forward to reveal the consequences a snapshot cannot see. It is positioned as the modular destination of the production system, not the first build.

## 7. Honest limitations to state

- **Crew dependencies are not modelled.** The system covers stands, size and zone, handler capacity, passport, baggage, and connections, but not crew availability or positioning. A production system would add crew as a resource and constraint.
- **Soft costs are not calibrated to business value.** They are relative points. The production step is to tie each to a real driver: passenger satisfaction from metres walked, available dwell and retail time, and schedule stability scored against the fees charged for stands. The trade-off is then negotiated in real terms.
- **A fault on an occupied stand is simplified.** An out-of-service stand that already has a parked aircraft is shown as out of service on the board; the aircraft already there is not separately handled.
- **The connection bank is illustrative.** Real connecting-passenger itineraries would replace the synthetic outbound mapping.
- **The Pareto corners are not calibrated.** The three strategies are genuine non-dominated solutions, but the spacing between the corners is not tuned. It follows from the uncalibrated soft costs, so the corners are directionally right but not magnitude-right.
- **The value figure uses a first-come-first-served baseline.** It measures the optimizer against the naive rule an incumbent would run, with assumed unit costs. It is a defensible business case, not an audited number, and it reads zero when the apron is uncontended.
- **The tow benefit needs contention to appear.** With the default short-dwell traffic no tow pays off, which is correct. The tow is demonstrated with an injected wide-body crunch that creates a real, fitting opportunity.
- **Towed aircraft do not reserve a remote position in the optimizer.** Aircraft assigned directly to a remote stand are capacity-constrained against each other, but a tow's parked phase is bounded only by the bus and tug fleet soft cost, not by a hard remote-apron-stand count. With many simultaneous tows and remote assignments this could exceed the physical remote positions. It does not bite at the demo's scale; production would add a remote-apron-capacity constraint.

## 8. The ML timing layer, the production approach

CPH is a fully implemented A-CDM airport (connected 2016-12-19), so the milestone data needed to train a timing model exists.

- **Training data.** Historical A-CDM milestones: actual in-block, actual off-block, target off-block, and turnaround durations, joined to the schedule.
- **Features.** Aircraft type, airline, stand and pier, ground handler, scheduled times, time of day and day of week, season, upstream network delay and inbound estimate, weather, and the preceding rotation's state.
- **Model.** Gradient-boosted trees (for example LightGBM) are the pragmatic choice for this tabular prediction.
- **Uncertainty.** Quantile regression (P10, P50, P90) or conformal prediction gives calibrated intervals, so the band is a learned and validated number rather than the fixed value the demo uses.

## 9. Standards and references

- Stand allocation and gate assignment are both provably NP-hard, which is why a solver, not a rule of thumb, is the right tool at scale.
- EU AI Act classification: most likely limited-risk, not Annex III high-risk. A read-only, advisory stand allocator is not a safety component of critical infrastructure, even though the airport is. The threshold to high-risk is autonomy: writing allocations back to Airhart or cancelling and diverting flights without a person. Transparency duties (Article 50) apply to the conversational interface, and GPAI obligations sit with the model provider (Microsoft), not the deployer. The position is to classify honestly as limited-risk and apply Article 14 oversight (disregard, override, reverse, stop) voluntarily. GDPR and Danish co-determination (works-council consultation) bind regardless of tier.
- EASA NPA 2025-07 covers Level 1 (assistance) and Level 2 (human-AI teaming), which is where this solution sits.
- The Microsoft reference architecture for contextual AI decision support specifies no auto-write and human review-and-accept, which the architecture follows.

## 10. The PoC versus full-scale line

The proof of concept and the full-scale demo prove the deterministic core and the human-in-the-loop loop today. The ML timing layer, the live integrations, and the conversational interface are the production build. The digital twin is the modular destination, the one tool that reaches the cascading half of the problem the snapshot cannot see. What remains for production is calibration (real units, real timings) and integration (live feeds), not a different method.

## 11. The full-scale build, capabilities

The full-scale app extends the deterministic core with five capabilities, each governed by a tunable policy constant in a Config tab. Every one defaults to off or neutral, so the verified base solve is unchanged until the governance owner enables it.

- **Governed config.** The hidden policy constants are surfaced in one place: the three objective weights, the escalation cost, the conflict buffer, the remote and oversize penalties, the tow controls and fleet sizes, the uncertainty and stability factors, and the euro unit costs. A change re-runs the optimization, so its effect is visible.
- **Counterfactual in the twin.** When a Pareto strategy is selected, the twin also projects the other two corners and a first-come-first-served baseline, so a consequence reads against the alternatives: "96 connections at risk under protect-schedule versus 40 under protect-passengers."
- **Tow-to-remote and fleet capacity.** A towable aircraft deboards on its contact stand, is towed to a remote parking position for the parked middle of its dwell, then returns to board, which frees the contact stand for another aircraft. The tow is a binary in the objective with a tow cost, and tug and bus fleet limits raise a soft cost when remote-serviced aircraft exceed the fleet. Gated behind a toggle and demonstrated with an injectable "wide-body parking crunch" scenario.
- **Uncertainty handling.** The conflict buffer can be sized from each flight's arrival-time uncertainty band, so tight-band flights pack closer and wide-band flights get more slack, visualised as a fuzzy edge on the Gantt bar. A Monte Carlo over a few hundred arrival-time draws reports how often the plan stays conflict-free ("holds in X% of scenarios") and flags fragile stands.
- **Bottom-up value.** A live euro figure against the first-come-first-served baseline an incumbent rule engine would run: protected connections (flights the rule strands that the optimizer operates), bus trips avoided, contact-stand-hours gained, less tow cost. Each driver times a configurable unit cost, labelled an assumption. The figure is zero when the optimizer and the baseline agree, and rises under disruption.
- **Rolling horizon.** A manual "advance and re-optimize" step re-solves from the committed picture with a stability term that keeps committed stands unless a move beats a penalty, so the plan does not thrash on every re-optimization. Production runs this on a short cadence against a time budget.

These are real computations on synthetic data. The fleet sizes, tow and uncertainty parameters, and unit costs are illustrative and tunable, not calibrated to CPH operations.

## 12. POC v4, the continuous optimizer

V4 turns the snapshot optimizer into a continuous one over a 48-hour feed. It lives in `poc-v4/index.html` (renamed from `full-scale/`); the app header and badge state "POC v4" and "Proof of concept". The V3 snapshot tabs are untouched; the V4 layer is the new "48h feed" and the realized-cost view of the Value tab.

- **48-hour feed and time engine.** A deterministic, seeded schedule of about 250 arrivals and their linked departures on 18 stands, with diurnal traffic, aircraft rotation chains, and early-turnaround delays that propagate ("Forsinkelser i tidlige turnarounds"). A clock plays it forward at one simulated minute per real second, scalable 0.1x to 10x, and the live picture is derived from the clock.
- **Continuous rolling-horizon optimization.** At a configurable cadence the apron is re-solved over a bounded window using the same glpk MIP idea. The decision set is bounded (the soonest not-yet-committed flights, capped) so each solve stays in the tens-of-milliseconds class; committed flights hold their stand as fixed occupancy and move only when it is lost. Tiered stability prices an on-ground move by the aircraft's state. Every reassignment is logged with a reason.
- **Six injectable disruptions.** Stand offline, handler-capacity cut, early or late arrival, departure delay, and longer turnaround, injected at the current feed time, applied as the clock passes them, self-clearing on duration, and recorded as a replayable set. A handler-capacity cut is logged and costed but, honestly, cannot be relieved by stand reallocation, because the handler is fixed per flight.
- **Continuous trade-off strategy control.** Five strategy presets feed the optimizer's weights, with a minimum-change cadence lock (no limit, 30 minutes, 1 hour, 2 hours) so the trade-off cannot thrash. Adaptive re-weights to the binding resource of the live window, cadence-limited.
- **Realized cost ledger.** The cost the run actually incurs, accrued event by event in sim-time, incurred only and never forecast: delay minutes, remote bus trips, strands, and gate-change reaccommodation. The V3 forward-looking four-case projection is kept as a separate sub-view. Runs can be saved (settings, disruption set, trace, total) and overlaid, so the optimize-off versus optimize-on gap is the realized value of optimizing.

The same caution applies and is stated in the app: this is a proof of concept on synthetic data. A production build would add real A-CDM and AODB data, a trained ML timing model with calibrated uncertainty, live Airhart integration with governed write-back, crew and equipment as modelled resources, calibrated euro units, and towing inside the continuous loop.
