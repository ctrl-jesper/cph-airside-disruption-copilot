The optimizer solves the apron one rolling window at a time as a binary assignment problem, with glpk.js (GLPK compiled to WebAssembly) running locally in the browser. Each x[flight,stand] is a 0/1 decision. Hard constraints are size code, stand availability, and no two aircraft overlapping in time on one stand. Soft costs, weighted by the current strategy, are passenger walking distance and connections, ground-handler balance, and schedule stability with remote-stand and zone penalties. This is one solve of the window at the feed clock: review the proposal and apply it to the live plan, or let the 48h feed run exactly this continuously on a cadence. Advisory here, autonomous there.

There is no single best allocation: protecting passenger walking distance worsens handler balance, and maximising contact-stand use sacrifices schedule stability. Here the window at the feed clock is solved four ways, balanced and with each objective protected in turn, and the resulting trade-off is charted. How this system sits under the EU AI Act, and what keeps it compliant in practice. The honest position is to classify it at the tier it actually occupies, apply the oversight of a higher tier voluntarily, and name the threshold that would move it up.

Limited‑risk
Most likely limited‑risk, not Annex III high‑risk. A read‑only, advisory stand allocator is not a safety component of critical infrastructure, even though an airport is critical infrastructure. The system proposes and explains; a person decides and accepts. It is decision support, not an automated safety function.

What applies at limited‑risk

  • Transparency duties (Article 50) on the conversational interface: people are told they are interacting with an AI, and AI‑generated content is identifiable.
  • GPAI obligations sit with the model provider, Microsoft, as the foundation‑model and Copilot Studio provider, not with the airport as the deployer.

The threshold to high‑risk

What would move this up is autonomy. Writing allocations back to Airhart automatically, or cancelling or diverting flights without a person, would make it a safety‑relevant automated component. Staying read‑only and advisory, with a human accepting every change, keeps it below that line. Article 14 human oversight is the design boundary: disregard, override, reverse, and stop.

What binds in practice, regardless of tier

  • GDPR. Passenger and connection records are personal data, so lawful basis, data minimisation, and retention limits apply. This demo runs on synthetic data only.
  • Danish co‑determination. A system that changes how duty managers and ground staff work is subject to works‑council consultation under the Danish model. Engage the works council early, not after deployment.

The position we take

Classify honestly as limited‑risk. Apply high‑risk‑grade oversight voluntarily: the Article 14 controls plus a full, replayable audit log of every proposal and decision. Name the threshold that would trigger high‑risk, and treat crossing it, write‑back or auto‑cancel, as a governance decision rather than a feature toggle.

Current posture in this build: read‑only, advisory, human‑in‑the‑loop. The system never allocates autonomously and never cancels or diverts a flight. Every committed assignment is revalidated continuously and logged in the Event log.

From this proof of concept to a production build

This is a POC on synthetic data: it demonstrates the method (a real MIP solver, a continuous rolling horizon, tiered stability, injectable disruptions, and a realized‑cost ledger) on a representative 48‑hour feed of 18 stands. A production build would add:

  • Real A‑CDM and AODB data in place of the synthetic feed, with live arrival, turnaround, and stand status.
  • A trained ML timing model with calibrated uncertainty bands, rather than the seeded prediction bands used here.
  • Live Airhart integration for the read picture, and governed, human‑accepted write‑back as a separate, later decision (the high‑risk threshold above).
  • Crew and equipment as modelled resources, so a ground‑handler shortage has a real lever rather than only a logged cost.
  • Calibrated euro cost units agreed with Finance and Operations, replacing the demonstration assumptions in Config.
  • Towing inside the continuous loop, extending the V3 tow model into the rolling optimizer.

The decision and the engine

The app re-allocates every disrupted flight across the whole apron at once, computes the result with a real optimizer, and presents it for a person to confirm. It is read-only and advisory.

The decision
Assign each flight that needs a stand to one stand, or escalate it if none fits. Stand allocation is the airport's own call. Divert and cancel stay with the airline, ATC, and the Network Manager.
Binary assignment (MIP)
Each flight-and-stand pairing is a yes-or-no decision, x[flight, stand] set to 1 or 0. A mixed-integer program picks the set of 1s that minimizes total cost while obeying the hard constraints. Solved by glpk.js, the GLPK solver compiled to WebAssembly, running locally in the browser.
Hard constraints
Rules that cannot be broken: a stand's size code must fit the aircraft, a stand that is out of service or already occupied at that time is unavailable, and no two aircraft may overlap in time on one stand.
Soft costs
Preferences, not rules. Every option carries a cost, and the solver minimizes the weighted total. These are the three objective scores below.
Escalate
If no stand can take a flight, the solver assigns an escalate option rather than forcing an illegal stand. That surfaces the flight as a redirect or cancel candidate for the airline and ATC. The airport never cancels a flight itself.

The three objective scores

Each score is the sum across all re-allocated flights. Lower is better. The numbers are dimensionless cost points, not minutes, meters, or kroner. Compare scores within one axis, never across axes: a passenger score of 294 is not "worse" than a handler score of 85.

Passenger score
How far passengers walk and how exposed their connections are. Per flight, (passengers / 100 × walking time) plus a connection-risk penalty when a remote stand carries tight connections. Higher when more passengers walk further, or connecting passengers land far out.
Handler score
Ground-handler strain. Per flight, handler load factor × remote-or-contact multiplier × flight size, plus the distance from that handler's apron base. Higher when a stretched handler services remote stands or stands far from its equipment.
Schedule score
Plan stability and contact-stand use. A fixed penalty of 40 per remote stand used, 8 per size code a stand is larger than the aircraft, and 25 for a forced zone change. Higher when the plan leans on remote stands, oversized stands, or zone changes.
On the unit. These are relative synthetic scores. They capture the shape of the trade-off correctly but not its real-world magnitude. In production they would be calibrated to real units, for example passenger connection-minutes lost, handler overtime-hours, schedule delay-minutes, or a euro cost, so the trade-off is expressed in terms operations and finance negotiate over. The optimization and the Pareto structure are real; the calibration is the next step.

Trade-offs and the Pareto front

The three objectives conflict, so there is no single best allocation. The solver lays out the genuine choices and leaves the judgment to a person.

Dominance
Allocation A dominates allocation B if A is at least as good as B on all three scores and strictly better on at least one. B is then strictly worse, a wasted option, so it is discarded.
Non-dominated allocation
One that nothing dominates. To improve any one score you must give up ground on another. These are the only options worth considering.
Pareto front
The full set of non-dominated allocations, the points plotted in the scatter. There is no mathematically best option among them. Dominance ranks bad options out, but cannot rank the survivors. Choosing one needs a human preference.
Signature strategies
The three extreme points on the front: lowest passenger score (protect passengers), lowest handler score (protect handlers), lowest schedule score (protect schedule).
Epsilon-constraint method
How the front is traced. Minimize one objective while capping the other two, then sweep the caps across a grid. Each solve returns one non-dominated point. This is the correct method for an integer program, where a simple weighted average would skip valid points.

Airport and operations terms

Size code (A to F)
ICAO aerodrome reference code by wingspan. A stand's maximum code must be equal to or larger than the aircraft's. An A320 is code C, a B777 is E, an A380 is F. R8 is the only code-F stand, so the EK151 A380 can only go there.
Contact vs remote
A contact stand has a jet bridge at the terminal. A remote stand sits on the apron and needs bus boarding plus extra handling, so it costs passengers and handlers more.
Schengen / non-Schengen
Passport-control routing. A Schengen flight on a non-Schengen contact stand, or the reverse, forces a zone change, which the schedule score penalizes. Remote stands are zone-neutral.
Baggage belt
Each contact pier feeds one reclaim belt; remote stands share the remote belt. Belt load is the bags currently feeding it.
Ground handler
The company servicing the turnaround (Aviator, Menzies, and SGH here), each with a capacity of simultaneous turnarounds. A handler near capacity is an operational risk.
Handler base
Each handler stages crews and equipment in an apron zone. Servicing a stand far from that base adds drive time and vehicle cycles, counted in the handler score as strain.
Dwell
Minutes a flight occupies a stand, from on-block to off-block.
Walking-time proxy
Minutes from a stand to central immigration and connections, used as the passenger-distance measure.
